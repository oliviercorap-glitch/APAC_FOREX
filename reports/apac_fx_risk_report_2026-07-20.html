Structural Background Note: Asia-Pacific FX Regimes & Implications for a Western Manufacturing Exporter

1. FX Regime Overview

| Currency | Regime Type | Key Characteristics | |----------|-------------|---------------------| | CNY | Managed float (crawling peg) | PBOC sets daily fixing (mid-point) against USD; spot trades within ±2% band. Band widened from ±1% in 2014. | | JPY | Free-floating | BOJ intervenes sporadically (typically via MoF orders) to counter disorderly moves, especially excessive weakness/strength. | | KRW | Free-floating | BOK intervenes via smoothing operations; known for "tolerance band" approach but no formal target. | | THB | Managed float | BOT intervenes to limit volatility; historically leans against excessive appreciation to support exports. | | PHP | Free-floating | BSP intervenes to manage volatility; often follows USD/Asia trends with occasional leaning against sharp moves. | | AUD | Free-floating | RBA does not target the exchange rate; intervention extremely rare (last major: 2008/09). |

2. Central Bank Mandates & Policy Tools

PBOC (China): Dual mandate: price stability + growth. Key tools: daily fixing, reserve requirement ratios (RRR), open market operations, macro-prudential measures (e.g., FX risk reserve requirement on forwards, counter-cyclical factor in fixing). The PBOC uses the fixing to signal policy intent and manage depreciation expectations.

BOJ (Japan): Price stability (2% inflation target). Tools: yield curve control (YCC) on 10-year JGBs, negative short-term policy rate (-0.1%), QQE (quantitative and qualitative easing). FX intervention is a MoF decision, executed by BOJ as agent.

BOK (Korea): Price stability + financial stability. Tools: base rate (7-day repo), FX smoothing operations, macro-prudential measures (e.g., limits on FX derivatives positions for banks). BOK has a floating rate but actively manages volatility.

BOT (Thailand): Monetary stability + financial system stability. Tools: policy rate, FX intervention (sterilised via BOT bonds), reserve requirements. BOT has historically accumulated reserves to cap THB strength.

BSP (Philippines): Price stability + financial stability. Tools: policy rate (reverse repo), reserve requirements, FX intervention (spot and forward). BSP allows flexibility but leans against sharp moves.

RBA (Australia): Price stability (2-3% inflation) + full employment. Tools: cash rate, QE (used 2020-21), forward guidance. RBA does not target AUD; rate decisions are the primary channel affecting AUD.

3. Structural Drivers vs. USD/EUR

Rate Differentials: The primary driver for JPY, KRW, THB, PHP, and AUD. When US/EU rates rise relative to Asia, capital outflows pressure these currencies. JPY is especially sensitive to US-Japan rate spreads given carry trade dynamics. AUD is sensitive to RBA vs. Fed/ECB rate expectations.

Capital Flows: Portfolio flows (equity and bond) dominate short-term moves for KRW, THB, PHP. China's capital account controls limit this for CNY, but trade flows and FDI are structural. AUD is driven by commodity price cycles and risk appetite (proxy for global growth).

Commodity Exposure (AUD): AUD is a proxy for iron ore, coal, LNG, and gold prices. A manufacturing exporter invoicing in AUD benefits when commodity prices rise (AUD strengthens) but faces headwinds when they fall.

Export Competitiveness Dynamics: THB and KRW are structurally sensitive to export competitiveness. BOT and BOK have historically intervened to prevent excessive appreciation that would hurt manufacturing exports. CNY is managed to maintain competitiveness, though the PBOC has allowed more two-way flexibility since 2015. PHP is less export-driven (remittances and BPO services are larger), so BSP is less concerned with competitiveness.

EUR/USD Cross: As your reporting currency is EUR, note that EUR/USD moves directly affect the EUR value of your USD-invoiced costs and revenues. A weaker EUR amplifies the EUR value of USD-denominated export receipts but also raises EUR-equivalent costs for USD-denominated inputs.

4. Practical Implications for a Manufacturing Exporter

Margin Exposure: You invoice in six currencies but report in EUR. This creates a multi-currency mismatch. The largest structural risk is likely CNY and JPY (largest trade volumes) and AUD (commodity-linked volatility). THB and PHP are smaller but can be volatile during risk-off episodes.

Typical Hedging Instruments: - Forward contracts: Most liquid for JPY, AUD, KRW; less liquid for THB and PHP (longer tenors may be costly). Use for firm commitments (e.g., confirmed orders). - Currency swaps: Useful for managing cash flow mismatches (e.g., receiving CNY in 3 months, needing EUR for payroll). Cross-currency swaps can hedge long-term exposures. - Natural hedging: Match invoicing currency with cost base where possible. For example, if you have CNY costs (e.g., Chinese labour or materials), invoicing in CNY reduces net exposure. Consider shifting some procurement to countries whose currencies match your invoicing.

Timing Considerations: - CNY: Fixing at 9:15am Beijing time sets the tone. Avoid executing large hedges during fixing window (volatility spikes). - JPY: Most liquid during Tokyo and London overlap (00:00-09:00 GMT). BOJ intervention typically occurs during Asian hours. - KRW, THB, PHP: Less liquid outside Asian hours; spreads widen during US/EU sessions. - AUD: Most liquid during Sydney/Tokyo overlap (22:00-06:00 GMT) and London morning.

Recommendation: Establish a rolling hedge programme (e.g., 3-6 month forward cover for 50-70% of expected net exposure) rather than spot-based hedging. Monitor central bank policy meetings (PBOC, BOJ, BOK, BOT, BSP, RBA) for rate decisions and intervention signals. Use options (collars, zero-cost structures) for currencies with high tail risk (JPY, AUD) to protect against extreme moves while allowing participation in favourable trends.
