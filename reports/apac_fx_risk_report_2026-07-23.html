Structural Background Note: APAC FX Regimes & Implications for a Western Manufacturing Exporter

1. FX Regime Overview

| Currency | Regime Type | Key Characteristics | |----------|-------------|---------------------| | CNY | Managed float (PBOC-controlled) | Daily fixing via “central parity” rate; spot trades within ±2% band. Band widened from ±1% in 2014. PBOC sets fixing based on prior close, basket weights (CFETS), and counter-cyclical factors. | | JPY | Free-floating | BOJ intervenes only sporadically (e.g., after disorderly moves). No explicit band. | | KRW | Free-floating | BOK occasionally smooths volatility via “smoothing operations” (sterilized intervention). No target zone. | | THB | Free-floating | BOT intervenes to curb excessive volatility or one-way bets, but no parity target. | | PHP | Free-floating | BSP intervenes to manage “excessive” volatility, often via spot market or FX swaps. | | AUD | Free-floating | RBA does not target the exchange rate; intervention extremely rare (last major: 2008). |

2. Central Bank Mandates & Policy Tools

  • PBOC (China): Dual mandate – price stability and growth. FX policy subordinated to financial stability. Tools: daily fixing, band adjustments, reserve requirement ratio (RRR) for FX deposits, forward reserve requirement (20% on banks’ FX forward positions), and verbal guidance (“counter-cyclical factor”).
  • BOJ (Japan): Price stability (2% inflation target). Tools: yield curve control (YCC) on 10y JGBs, negative policy rate (-0.1%), QQE (asset purchases). FX intervention is MoF-led, not BOJ, but BOJ executes it.
  • BOK (Korea): Price stability + financial stability. Tools: base rate (currently restrictive cycle), FX swap lines with NPS (National Pension Service), and verbal intervention.
  • BOT (Thailand): Inflation targeting (1–3% core CPI). Tools: policy rate, FX intervention (sterilized via BOT bonds), and capital flow management measures (e.g., bond holding limits for non-residents).
  • BSP (Philippines): Price stability + financial stability. Tools: policy rate, RRR, FX intervention (spot and swaps), and reserve requirement on foreign currency deposits.
  • RBA (Australia): Price stability (2–3% inflation) + full employment. Tools: cash rate, QE (used 2020–21), and forward guidance. No FX target.

3. Structural Drivers vs. USD/EUR

Rate Differentials

  • JPY: Ultra-low rates (negative or near-zero) vs. USD/EUR → persistent carry-driven depreciation pressure. BOJ’s YCC anchors long-end yields, widening rate differentials.
  • CNY: PBOC policy rate (1y LPR ~3.45%) vs. Fed/ECB rates. Narrowing differentials in 2023–24 have reduced carry appeal. Capital controls limit arbitrage.
  • KRW, THB, PHP: Policy rates broadly tracking Fed/ECB cycles, but with lags. Higher domestic inflation often forces tighter policy, supporting currencies temporarily.
  • AUD: RBA cash rate (currently ~4.35%) offers moderate carry vs. USD, but AUD is more sensitive to commodity prices and risk appetite than rate differentials.

Capital Flows

  • CNY: Dominated by trade flows (large surplus) and portfolio flows (equity/bond). PBOC manages capital account liberalization cautiously; “hot money” flows are limited.
  • JPY: Large current account surplus (trade + investment income) provides structural support, but portfolio outflows (Japanese investors seeking yield abroad) weigh on JPY.
  • KRW, THB, PHP: Vulnerable to global risk sentiment (risk-on/risk-off). Equity and bond flows are volatile; carry trades amplify moves.
  • AUD: High correlation with global equity markets and commodity prices (iron ore, coal, LNG). AUD is a “risk currency” – strengthens in risk-on, weakens in risk-off.

Commodity Exposure (AUD)

  • AUD is the only pure commodity currency in the group. Terms of trade (export prices vs. import prices) drive long-run AUD/USD. China’s demand for Australian resources is a key driver.

Export Competitiveness Dynamics

  • CNY: PBOC historically undervalued CNY to support export sector, but since 2015 has allowed more flexibility. Exporters now hedge more actively.
  • JPY: Weak JPY benefits exporters (Toyota, Sony) but hurts importers (energy, food). BOJ tolerates weakness as long as it does not cause financial instability.
  • KRW, THB, PHP: Competitive depreciation is a concern; central banks intervene to prevent excessive weakening that would fuel inflation (imported) or destabilize capital flows.

4. Practical Implications for a Manufacturing Exporter

Margin Exposure from Invoicing Currency Mismatches

  • Revenue in CNY, JPY, KRW, THB, PHP, AUD.
  • Costs likely in EUR (head office, European inputs) and USD (commodities, logistics).
  • Reporting in EUR → EUR appreciation reduces reported revenue; EUR depreciation boosts it.
  • Key risk: If JPY/CNY weaken vs. EUR, EUR-denominated margins compress. If AUD strengthens, margins improve.

Typical Corporate Hedging Instruments

  • Forward contracts: Most common for 3–12 month horizons. Lock in exchange rate for future invoices. Available for all six currencies vs. EUR and USD.
  • Currency swaps: Used for longer-term exposures (1–5 years) or to manage balance sheet mismatches (e.g., CNY debt vs. EUR revenue).
  • Natural hedging: Match revenue and cost currencies. Example: source raw materials in AUD if invoicing in AUD; pay Chinese suppliers in CNY if invoicing in CNY.
  • Options: For uncertain cash flows (e.g., tender-based sales). Costly but provide downside protection while allowing upside.

Timing Considerations

  • CNY: PBOC fixing at 9:15am Beijing time; spot moves within band. Best to execute hedges after fixing to avoid intraday volatility.
  • JPY: Most liquid during Tokyo/London overlap (7am–9am GMT). BOJ intervention risk is highest during Asian hours.
  • KRW, THB, PHP: Less liquid; wider spreads. Hedge during local market hours (9am–4pm local time) for best pricing.
  • AUD: Most liquid during Sydney/London overlap (10pm–7am GMT). RBA announcements (first Tuesday of month, except January) can cause sharp moves.

Recommendation

  • Policy: Hedge 50–70% of forecasted FX exposure for 6–12 months forward, using a rolling program. Adjust hedge ratio based on currency volatility and central bank intervention risk.
  • Monitoring: Track PBOC fixing, BOJ YCC decisions, BOK/BOT/BSP policy meetings, and RBA rate decisions. Combine with recent FX data (separate analysis) to adjust tactical hedges.
